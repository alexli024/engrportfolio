Name Tag

Link to model from class
I followed the class instructions to print my first 3D printing product. The name tag came out really well. My printer I used is a Prusa Mini+ with 20% infill and 0.2mm nozzle. I used white and green PLA. I added my name in the Prusa Slicer and moved it to the center of the name tag. I exported the gcode then I took it to a Prusa Mini+ with a flashdrive.

This is the name tag sitting on the Prusa Mini+. It is a dual-color print and I learned to unload/load filament from Professor Mike. Overall the name tag came out terrific. This print was the perfect basic introduction to 3D printing for me.

Stress Test

Link to model on Printables.com
I chose a very complex stress test. This model has a lot of overhang and archs. I sliced the model at 10% infill, without support or brim to test the Prusa Mini+ that I was working with. To ensure that I test the printer correctly, I asked Robert to calibrate the printed beforehand. As the image displays, the print was successful until the middle of the statue, where the overhang items start to print. I believe a previous portion of the structure fell and the rest of the print was sprayed out into thin air. The filament was still connected to the feet of the statue so the weight dragged the legs off the base so the whole thing collapsed. The Prusa slicer warned about floating obejct parts, bridge anchors, thin fragile parts, and loose extrusions. Looking back I could have used organic support and a brim should I wanted to ensure the quality of the print over stress testing.

TinkerCAD Model Meshing

Link to model of the Rock's head.
Link to model of the elephant.
My proudest creation yet - the Rocklephant. I developed this model using TinkerCAD. I found both models on Printables.com and the idea popped into my head. I removed the head and trunk of the elephant using TinkerCAD's basic hole function. And I aligned the Rock's head with the elephant's body. I should note this is the project in which I learned how to activate organic support. I had found the organic support setting in Printer Setting tab this whole time but I never turned it on.

The print came out very well. The support came off very naturally. Organic support seems to be a much viable option than snug support. TinkerCAD is very intuiive to use and I look forward to creating more models in TinkerCAD.

TinkerCAD Model Meshing

Link to model of the moon.
Link to model of the dragon.
Originally I was modifying my TinkerCAD model, the rocklephant, in Fusion360 to create a brand new object. But Fusion360 keeps crashing due to hardware limitations on my laptop. I changed my mind and looked for new ideas. I came up with a design of a handrest that has somekind of round object for the palm, and a part that extends off the ball for the forearm. I was worried about the fragility so I turned up the infill to 20%. I didn't have exact models in mind and I came aross the perfect round shaped moon model on Thingiverse.com. I found another dragon model that is already in a crawling position. After importing them as meshes into Fusion360, I modified the size and orientation to make sure them match and the tail of the dragon extends off the moon.

The print came out pretty good. There was a small hiccup where the printer broke the first time and I was worried that it's the model's fault. But the second print came out smoothly. I decided to keep the support "trees" because it looks cool and I'm worried that the tail wouldn't be able to support the weight of my arm. I should have added claws/legs to the dragon looking thing so it has more support.

Basic E-Nable Hand

This project was designed to learn e-Nable hub resources as well as familiarizing myself with 3d printed prosthetics. I had the pleasure of working with Chung for this project. We both arranged our own printable models in Ideamaker at 150%, mine is pictured above. We ended up using leftover parts from previous years to assemble our hand, which is why our model is multi-coloured. It worked out perfectly and I like the colour combination.

Chang and I bent the wrist piece of the heat gun pinned in the front of the classroom with a temperature of 405 degrees Fahrenheit. We had some trouble with shaping the curve with the pink bludgeon. I ended up shaping the wrist support by heating it up from all angles then pressing it onto a preexisting wrist mold while wearing gloves. Unfortunately we don’t have a video of that because we were focused on not being burnt.
I watched two of the instructional videos on Latte to begin assembling. We first connected the thumb then finger joints to the main palm, following the instructions. We had planned on hot gluing the knots since the beginning, but I still made several knots in different styles to make sure the hot glue has enough “real estate” to stick to. We put on one rubber finger tip to make sure the hot glue doesn’t interfere with it. We tied the tendons to the tension adjuster screws as instructions intended. To make sure the length works out perfectly, we didn’t cut the string until the very end. That added about 5 mins to our work time but was very worth it because it helped avoid us running out of string before we finished each finger section. We finished the assembling process by measuring and cutting the foam padding on the inside. The finished product worked really well. It can lift fairly sized objects, such as a screwdriver. ' We did some more testing but couldn’t get an estimate of the upper limit of the load.

For the next part of the project, I wanted to add a very simple function to hold pencils on the back of the palm. My initial idea was to simply add a “inventory space” where the user can put anything necessary, but considering the weight and maneuverability of the hand, I opted for 3 simple pencil clips on the back of the palm. As the picture below shows, it's a very simple design and I drew it together in Fusion. The clips are about 6 mm in diameter, which is the standard diameter of pencils according to CW Pencil Enterprise. The clips use PLA which is soft enough to snap in the pencils without breaking them.

Problem

For this project, we are identifying the problem that we want to tackle and initiate a moderate level of research around the design and pre-existing models that may be similar.
The problem we are trying to solve is low functionality within the metacarpal section of the hand limb. We are targeting individuals who are missing body parts past their metacarpal bones. This device would require enough palm section left to successfully attach. The WHO provides a global estimate of 35-40 mm people who require prosthetics. In the United States, about 35000 people have amputations at the hand currently, and 0.009% of births are with an absent hand or fingers. For an estimation with a very large margin, using an estimated 4mm/yr birth rate in the US, a current population of 336 million (U.S. Census Bureau), hand amputation rate of 0.02 per 100000 people (Science Direct), there’s about 0.02/100000*336000000=67.2 new cases per year related to trauma plus a congenital absent birth of 360 per year. We hope to restore partially some of their daily functions through our universal adaptor and the various tools that come with it.

We took some inspiration from this this Ford and Spoon support model. We’d like to design an universal adaptor similar to this model but stronger as in load and durability. Furthermore, we will expand the range of functions of this adaptor by introducing universal adapters that can be easily switched. This modular tool system will range across the field of manufacturing and construction, these are tools with interchangeable heads with varying functionality such as drilling, cutting, and sanding. This customizability aspect is a key aspect in what we want to achieve with our product. At the moment, we are seeking individuals with one functioning hand and one limited hand mobility with enough palm section left to fit e-nable hand. In the nature of e-nable hands, these individuals would need to have function wrist movement. We have no age limit as we can modify the size of the hand and adaptor as needed. This device is for individuals who are struggling with mundane tasks and aim to restore some functionality.
Next up we found another approach to the problem that could handle something heavier, as demonstrated by the picture below. This is not a printed model but rather a fabricate glove. That being said, we took inspirations as to how to increase the strength of the joint of our prototype.
We'll be conducting internal product testing since we're not working with a specific user at the moment. Our testing environment will mirror the user profile we've previously outlined. To ensure clarity in our testing process, we'll opt for a quantitative approach to assess the effectiveness of our product. Our focus will be on evaluating the structural integrity of the product, examining both the clip mechanism and the tool linkage mechanism. Our measurements will target:

1. The force necessary to compromise the curved segment of the clip mechanism.
2. The force required to adjust the angle of the ball joint when the nut is securely fastened.


After completing these tests, we'll move on to prototype production. The final design selected as successful will be the one demonstrating the highest level of structural resilience which we will test with a force gauge once available.
